The film industry uses specific numerical designations to describe shots
containing distinct counts of actors. These are essential for blocking scenes
and ensuring consistent eyelines.

One Shot (or Single) This term refers to a shot containing only one subject.
While often implied by shot sizes like "Close-Up" or "Medium Shot" when focused
on a single character, explicitly calling for a "One Shot" ensures no other
characters enter the frame. It is frequently used for isolation, introspection,
or when a character is speaking alone.

Two Shot (2S) This frames two subjects in the same shot.  It is the standard
shot for establishing a relationship, conversation, or physical interaction
between two characters. The framing can vary (e.g., a "Full Two Shot" shows both
bodies entirely, while a "Medium Two Shot" shows them from the waist up), but
the defining feature is the presence of exactly two figures sharing the space
equally or in direct relation to one another.

Three Shot (3S) This term describes a frame containing three subjects.  It is
commonly used in scenes involving a trio of characters, such as a family unit, a
group of friends, or a confrontation involving three parties. Like the Two Shot,
it emphasizes the group dynamic and the spatial relationship between all three
individuals simultaneously, rather than cutting between individual reactions


Specialized Multi-Subject Framings When the number of subjects exceeds three, or
when the arrangement is specific, other terms come into play to describe the
composition relative to the group count.

Group Shot (or Ensemble Shot) This is the general term for a frame containing
four or more subjects.  Unlike the specific numerical terms (One, Two, Three),
"Group Shot" covers any configuration where a collective is the focus. This is
vital for scenes depicting crowds, teams, families, or gatherings where the
collective reaction is more important than individual details.

Crowd Shot Distinct from a Group Shot, a Crowd Shot implies a large, often
indistinct number of people where individual faces may not be recognizable or
important. The focus here is on the mass, the scale of the gathering, or the
atmosphere created by the multitude, rather than specific interactions between
counted individuals.

1½ Shot (or One and a Half Shot) This is a nuanced term describing a composition
with one primary subject in focus (usually in the foreground) and a second
subject partially visible or out of focus in the background.  It is technically
a two-person shot, but the framing prioritizes one character while acknowledging
the presence of the other, often used to show eavesdropping or a looming threat
without shifting the narrative focus entirely.


Over-the-Shoulder (OTS): While it
technically contains parts of two people, it is functionally a One Shot of the
person facing the camera, using the other person's shoulder merely as a framing
device.  It is rarely counted as a "Two Shot" in script breakdowns because the
focus is singular. Dirty Singles: This is a variation of the One Shot where a
small part of another character (like the back of a head or shoulder) is visible
in the edge of the frame. It maintains the focus on one subject while subtly
implying the proximity of another, bridging the gap between a clean Single and a
Two Shot.
